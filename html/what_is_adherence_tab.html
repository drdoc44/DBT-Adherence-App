Types of Adherence

Hierarchy (reference Table 6.2)

"A DBT therapist, as an individual’s primary therapist, has the responsibility to organize each therapy session by treatment goals for that particular stage of therapy. Targeting in DBT refers to the therapists’ ability to organize each session by four classes of behaviors ordered by priority: Suicidal Behaviors, Therapy-interfering behaviors, Quality-of-life-interfering behaviors, Increasing behavioral skills. "



Dialectical (reference Table 7.1)

"In each session, therapist strive to balance treatment strategies of acceptance (i.e, validation) with change (i.e., problem-solving)."



Dialectical Thinking (reference Table 7.1)

"Therapist models dialectical thinking by searching for a synthesis, examining both sides of a position, ask “what is left out”. Therapist make use of techniques such as speaking in metaphor’s, Devil’s Advocate, and WISE MIND to help the patient come to his/her own conclusions."



Reciprocal Communication (reference Table 12.1)

"Responsiveness to the patient includes warm engagement, self-disclosure, and is genuine and nonjudgmental. Therapist demonstrated an awareness of the power balance in the relationship relative to their role as therapist. "



Irreverent Communication (reference Table 12.2)

"Irreverent communication is meant to be genuine and come from a place of compassion. Therapist discuss dysfunctional behavior in a matter-of-fact manner and directly confronts dysfunctional behavior. Therapist can, at times, use humor or an off-kilter style to keep the client off-balance while ensuring they have sufficient time to rebalance."



V2- Validation Through Accurate Reflection (reference Table 8.1-8.3)

"Using a nonjudgmental stance, therapists summarizes what the patient is sharing with statements such as “Let me make sure I’m understanding you correctly, you said…” A therapist does not have to agree with the patient when reflecting but simply is attempting to demonstrate understanding."



V5- Validation Through Communicating That Feelings Are Valid (reference Table 8.1-8.3)

"The therapist is responsible to communicating that the patient’s subjective emotional reactions is valid. The therapist should normalize what the patient is experiencing based on the immediate, present, context. This can be used as a learning-based explanation for the patient’s emotional responses."



Behavioral Analysis (reference Table 9.1)

"Behavioral analysis is the first step to help the patient start problem-solving. Behavioral Analysis starts by attempting to figure out what the problem is, then looks to identify the antecedents and consequences following the problematic behavior. Therapist is careful to use behavioral language by highlighting the distinction between thoughts, feelings, and behaviors."



Insight (reference Table 9.2)

"The therapist works to assist the patient in identifying patterns of behavior associated with the identified problem. Specifically, the therapist aids the patients to observe and describe recurrent behavior, highlighting patterns as they arise inside and outside of session, and explore the implications."



Solution Analysis (reference Table 9.4)

"Therapist and client collaboratively brainstorm solutions, evaluate solutions, and choose a solution. Therapist spends sufficient time to plan a homework assignment and trouble-shoots the HW."



Session Ending Strategies (reference Table 14.4)

Therapists end the session with sufficient time to conduct a winddown or WRAP around procedure. Therapist spend the final minutes of each summarizing the homework and progress made in session as well as addressing any last-minute concerns or issues. ""



"The definitions for the information provided are drawn from Linehan (1993). Linehan, M. M. (1993). Diagnosis and treatment of mental disorders: Cognitive-behavioral treatment of borderline personality disorder. New York, NY: Guilford Press."
